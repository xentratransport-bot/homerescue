Why NYC Has a Mold Problem

New York City has one of the highest rates of residential mold in the country. The causes are structural and climatic: aging building stock with poor ventilation, high ambient humidity, aging plumbing that leaks chronically, and the simple reality of millions of people living in dense, often poorly-maintained apartments.

NYC's housing stock includes buildings with inadequate vapor barriers, outdated insulation, and plumbing systems that have been patched and re-patched for decades. A pipe that leaked six months ago and was "fixed" by painting over the water stain may have left a thriving mold colony inside the wall cavity. The mold continues growing, releasing spores into the air your family breathes every day.

NYC Mold Laws: What Tenants and Landlords Need to Know

New York City has some of the most comprehensive mold legislation in the country, established under Local Law 55 of 2018. Key provisions include:

  • Landlord responsibility: Property owners must remediate mold when affected areas exceed 10 square feet. They must hire licensed mold assessors and remediation contractors, and they cannot rent units with unaddressed mold conditions.
  • Licensed professionals required: For areas over 10 square feet, NYC requires both a licensed mold assessment contractor and a separate licensed mold remediation contractor to perform the work (they cannot be the same company for projects over this threshold).
  • Retaliation prohibition: Landlords cannot retaliate against tenants who report mold, request repairs, or contact city agencies about mold conditions.
  • Tenant rights: Tenants can report mold to 311, file complaints with the NYC Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD), and pursue rent abatement in housing court for persistent uncorrected conditions.

Health Effects of Mold Exposure in NYC

Mold exposure affects approximately 25% of the population more severely than others, particularly those with mold allergies, asthma, compromised immune systems, or genetic predispositions to mycotoxin sensitivity. However, any significant mold exposure in a home environment poses health risks:

  • Chronic respiratory symptoms — cough, wheezing, shortness of breath
  • Sinus congestion and post-nasal drip
  • Eye, nose, and throat irritation
  • Skin rashes
  • Headaches and fatigue that improve when away from home (a key indicator of household mold)
  • In high concentrations of toxigenic molds: more serious neurological and respiratory effects

The HomeRescue Mold Remediation Process

Our remediation process follows IICRC S520 standard for mold remediation and complies with NYC Local Law 55 requirements:

  • Assessment: Licensed inspector evaluates visible mold, conducts air sampling, and uses moisture mapping to identify all affected areas and moisture sources.
  • Remediation plan: We provide a detailed scope of work before any demolition or removal begins.
  • Containment: Plastic barriers and negative air pressure machines prevent cross-contamination during work.
  • Removal: Affected porous materials (drywall, insulation, wood) are bagged and removed. Non-porous surfaces are HEPA-vacuumed and treated with antimicrobial agents.
  • HEPA air scrubbing: Commercial air scrubbers with HEPA filters clean the air during and after remediation.
  • Moisture source correction: We address or coordinate repair of the underlying moisture source — without fixing the leak, mold will return.
  • Reconstruction: We rebuild removed walls, ceilings, and floors with mold-resistant materials (purple drywall, antimicrobial insulation).
  • Clearance testing: Post-remediation air sampling verifies successful remediation. You receive a clearance certificate.
